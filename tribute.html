A brief history of Stephen Hawking: A legacy of paradox


Stephen Hawking, the world-famous theoretical physicist, has died at the age of 76

Beginning with the big bang

Hawking was diagnosed with amyotrophic lateral sclerosis, a degenerative motor neurone disease that quickly robs people of the ability to voluntarily move their muscles. He was told he had two years to live. Although Hawking’s body may have weakened, his intellect stayed sharp. Hawking had his own scientific vision to investigate the beginning of time.

Time’s arrow

Hawking was studying the work of Roger Penrose, which proved that if Einstein’s general theory of relativity is correct, at the heart of every black hole must be a point where space and time themselves break down – a singularity. Hawking realised that if time’s arrow were reversed, the same reasoning would hold true for the universe as a whole. Under Sciama’s encouragement, he worked out the maths and was able to prove it: the universe according to general relativity began in a singularity.

Thermodynamic threat

The second law of thermodynamics is one of the most well-established laws of nature. It states that the entropy, or level of disorder in a system, always increases. The second law gives form to the observation that ice cubes will melt into a puddle, but a puddle of water will never spontaneously turn into a block of ice. All matter contains entropy, so what happens when it is dropped into a black hole? Is entropy lost along with it? If so, the total entropy of the universe goes down and black holes would violate the second law of thermodynamics.

Bekenstein and breakthrough

In 1972, at a physics summer school in France, Stephen Hawking encountered Jacob Bekenstein, a graduate student who proposed that the second law of thermodynamics, specifically the concept of entropy, should apply to black holes. Bekenstein suggested that the growth of a black hole's event horizon as it consumes matter could represent an increase in entropy. Initially, Hawking opposed the idea, believing it flawed. However, after attempting to disprove it, he instead confirmed the relationship between entropy and a black hole's horizon, leading to one of his greatest scientific breakthroughs.

Hawking Radiation

Stephen Hawking embraced the idea that black holes could have entropy and therefore emit radiation, a concept he initially resisted. By combining general relativity with quantum mechanics, he discovered that black holes emit what is now known as Hawking radiation. This occurs when particle-antiparticle pairs form near the event horizon, and one escapes while the other is captured by the black hole. Although difficult to experimentally confirm, Hawking radiation is widely accepted as his most significant scientific contribution.

Theory of everything

The Bekenstein-Hawking entropy equation, which unites constants from gravity, quantum mechanics, relativity, and thermodynamics, was engraved on Stephen Hawking's tombstone, symbolizing his pursuit of a unified theory of physics. However, his discovery that black holes can radiate and eventually evaporate led to the black hole information loss paradox, which challenges key principles of quantum mechanics and relativity. In 1976, Hawking controversially argued that black holes do lose information when they evaporate, sparking a debate that continues to be one of the most significant challenges in theoretical physics.

Concession

In 2004, he dramatically reversed his stance at a conference in Dublin, declaring that black holes cannot lose information. This issue, central to the quest for quantum gravity, remains unresolved, with new paradoxes like the firewall emerging. Although Hawking's most influential work was in the 1970s, his later efforts in quantum cosmology and his unique problem-solving methods continued to push the boundaries of theoretical physics.

Hawking the superstar

His genre-defining book, A Brief History of Time, has sold more than 10 million copies since its publication in 1988, and has been translated into more than 35 languages. He was a world-leading theoretical physicist, clearly the best in the world for his time among those working at the intersection of gravity and quantum mechanics, and he did it all in the face of a terrible disease. He is an inspirational figure, and history will certainly remember him that way.
